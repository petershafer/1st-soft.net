Percolating into the Past

Coffee and Culture through the Ages

Goats, Caffeine, and Ethiopian Coffee

Coffee’s history is no less rich than its intoxicating flavor. The potent picker-upper appears to have its beginnings in early Ethiopia.1 Legend has it that while feeding his goats one day, a herder observed them dancing as if bewitched. He then noticed some of the herd chewing on a local tree’s red beans and leaves. Curious, he timidly popped a few berries into his mouth, becoming the first human to experience a coffee-induced caffeine rush. Happy and hyper, he joined his goats in their bizarre dance.2

Though this early account has a blatant lack of factual evidence, it does capture the spirit with which coffee was received by the early world. The Ethiopians always looked on coffee with magical wonder. Perhaps as early as 600 AD did these beans serve as a quick energy fix. But it was not until the the late 15th or early 16th century that humankind adopted the process of roasting, grinding, and brewing to produce today’s palatable drink.1

Laws, Lewd behavior, and Lavish Sacraments: Coffee in the Middle-East

After coffee drinking came to be a ceremonial practice in Ethiopia, it quickly caught on in the Middle East. Arabs cultivated coffee to stave off sleep during midnight prayers.2 Even fortune tellers used coffee to bolster their clairvoyance. During grind reading, a teller would analyze the residue in a finished cup of coffee. The fortune teller would then predict clients' health and happiness by the pattern of their grinds.3

Coffee also served as an excuse to socialize. In 1475, Kiva Han, the first coffee shop in the world, opened in Constantinople.4 To say the least, it was very well-received. By the 16th century, coffeehouses sprang up throughout the Arabian peninsula, bringing together the masses for candor, conversation, and mischief. These shops' ambiance, however, differed greatly from today's café atmosphere.

Gambling, criminal activity, and lewd sexual practices became commonplace in the early coffee scene.1 Troubled by what they understood to be off-color behavior, political leaders enacted a series of laws upon the beverage and its abode. In 1511, governor Khair-Beg closed Mecca’s coffeehouses and totally banned the brew. Shortly thereafter, Sultan Amurat III, so afraid of subversive activity in early coffeehouses, had every coffee shop closed and its owners tortured!4 Still, underground drinkers persisted, and by the time the Ottoman Empire occupied Arab lands, coffee emerged as one of the Turks’ most profitable exports.1

A new, less taboo type of coffeehouse replaced the risqué establishments throughout Turkey. Unlike their predecessors, these Middle-Eastern coffee shops served as fashionable men’s clubs, where members satiated their appetites for intellectual and artistic conversation. In fact, many scholars believe these coffee clubs to be forerunners of the Paris cafés.3

With its explosion in popularity came a wealth of new coffee customs. From roasting the beans to serving guests, the Turks ritualized coffee preparation. Even today many Turkish girls still brew and serve coffee for prospective in-laws to display their domestic skills.3 The drink was so vital to their lifestyle that Turkish law permitted a woman to legally divorce her husband if he could not provide her with a daily coffee quota.4

Indeed, the Ottomans prized the coffee industry so much that they forbade any unroasted bean to leave the country. For years, the Turks’ carefully guarded monopoly made transplantation to other lands impossible. Finally, in the early 17th century, a Muslim traveler managed to tape a few beans to his belly and sneak them out of the border.1

These beans took well to the fertile grounds of India. Determined to bring the coffee bean to their land, the Dutch transplanted one healthy tree to the Netherlands in 1616. This move brought coffee to the West and heralded a soon-to-be coffee craze in Europe.1

Coffee's Sweet Spot in Europe

Though initially believed to be “the devil’s brew” in the West, coffee soon fell into good esteem. Pope Clementine took to the drink so well that he encouraged Europeans to baptize it as a “Christian beverage” to shirk off its Middle-Eastern heritage.1

In 1673, the first European-built coffeehouse opened its doors in Bremen, Germany. Another opened in Hamburg only four years later, and within a decade, coffee shops spread throughout the German countryside. Coffee even found its way into the German city of Vienna after the Turks' raid. When the foreign troops retreated, they left behind 500 sacks of raw coffee beans, piquing Vienna's interest. This failed military move marked the beginning of world-renown Viennese coffee culture.5

When coffee first arrived in Vienna, the Turkish brew's bitter taste first turned off the Viennese. To accommodate for their consumers' sweet teeth, coffeehouses began to add a variety of sugary substances to make their drink more palatable.1 One popular early concoction was the Vienna Melange, which was a Turkish brew mixed with cream and honey. When these and other sweet ingredients became commonplace in cafés, coffee culture surged in Vienna, rendering it one of the most distinct in all the world.5

Coffeehouses worked their way to Italy in 1683, when the doors of Venice’s caffé first opened. England and France soon followed suit, and by the early 18th century, cafés became synonymous with brilliant minds, artistic pursuits, and great food. Balzac, Beethoven, and Voltaire were just a few prominent coffeehouse regulars. Johann Sebastian Bach even performed his Coffee Cantata in a local shop.5 It was, indeed, café conversation that helped fuel and organize the French Revolution.1 What is now considered to be a pedestrian drink forever changed the face of Europe. Who dares to guess how many intellectual, artistic, and heroic accomplishments would never have seen existence were it not for a hot cup of joe.

Perking up the Daily Grind: Coffee Comes to the Americas

After its warm reception in Europe, it was only a matter of time until coffee became commonplace to American life. Once the Europeans introduced coffee to Latin America, many natives started cultivating the bean on their own estates. Due to its superior transportation system and use of slave labor, Brazil’s coffee production dominated world markets. In fact, Brazil had such a large influence in coffee production that until the end of World War II, it supplied three quarters of the world coffee market.4 Because of these new plantations in Central and South America, coffee, for the first time, became a beverage for all classes.1

By the 1870’s, Americans were consuming six times more coffee than Europeans. This “indispensable beverage” emerged as a 20th century staple of American life and has gained an even greater following throughout the 21st. While in America, coffee boomed after the industrial revolution, coffeehouses remained a rarity for many years. Not until the 1970’s did cafés hit it big in the U.S..1

Specialty shop coffees and exotic roasts offered Americans an alternative to pre-ground, tin-sealed beans. The 1990’s were a particularly prosperous age for the bean, spawning coffee giants such as Starbucks and Green Mountain.1 By the rise of the 21st century, coffee acquired such a following that it could now be found in thousands of flavors and varieties almost anywhere on earth.

History demonstrates that craving coffee is more than just a caffeine addiction; as a café drink it helps satiate our thirst for creativity, community, controversy, and change. Whether to fan the flames of a revolution, feed a groggy brain, or fuel an all-nighter, coffee will continue to refill our cups and reawaken our minds.
